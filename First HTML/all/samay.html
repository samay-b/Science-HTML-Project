"New" Terms to Remember

Force is a push or pull.
Motion is the action of an object changing location.
Vectors are quantities of direction and magnitude.
Velocity is a vector of speed and direction.
Acceleration is the absolute change of velocity.
Momentum is the quantity of mass and velocity.
Mass is the amount of matter an object contains.
Gravity is the force attracting a physical body with mass.
Friction is the force resisting motion due to rubbing.
Our world is full of forces. Forces allow us to walk, eat, blink, sleep, and much more. Motion is related to force, in which that any slight change of position due to force causes it. Gravity is a force which allows a mass to attract itself closer. Earth, as you know, contains gravity, which is the reason why when we jump, we move back down. A mass is anything that contains matter, or space, which even includes air! In physics, a vector is considered a quantity of direction and magnitude, or amount, which should be able to be shown by an arrow. Velocity is an example of a vector, which contains of speed and direction, which is representable by an arrow. The constant change of velocity, or speed and direction, is called acceleration (or decceleration). Compared to running, you can stop anytime while walking. Momentum states that an object's mass and velocity will determine the required amount of force to stop it. Friction is the force which slows masses down by contact, and as a result, heat is created.

Issac Newton's Three Laws of Motion:

First Law: The Law of Inertia.

Inertia states that an object at rest tends to stay at rest, and an object in motion continues to stay in motion unless an outside force interferes its original state of motion.

Second Law: Force=Mass*Acceleration.

This law displays the relationship between force, mass, and acceleration. Massive objects require more force to stop it, and high acceleration means that a greater force is needed to stop the object.

Third Law: Actions and Reactions

What this law means is that for every action, such as kicking a ball, or punching the air, an equal and opposite reaction takes place. Ex: The harder you kick the ball, the farther it'll soar.
